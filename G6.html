Availability, Limitations, Ethics, and Societal Impact Statement

Food Safety QR Code and App Project

Availability

Our food safety information system is designed to make food-related safety information easier for ordinary consumers to access, understand, and reuse. Each food item, such as fruit, vegetables, meat, seafood, or packaged food, would include a QR code that links users to a product information page in our app. This page would show key information such as product origin, producer or supplier, processing location, transportation path, inspection status, recall warnings, allergen information, expiration date, and basic storage guidance. The goal is not only to store food safety data, but also to make that data transparent to users for making food decisions.

The project supports availability through multiple access methods. Consumers can scan a QR code with a phone, search for a food product in the app, or access a web version if they do not want to install the app. For users with limited technical knowledge, the system will lead users to a web page use simple labels such as “Safe,” “Needs Attention,” or “Recall Warning,” while still allowing advanced users to view the original data sources and detailed records. This follows the open data idea that broader access can increase public benefit, but access should be paired with clear documentation and responsible design. Locke and Weber explain that open data ethics often involves balancing transparency, fairness, utility, and rule compliance rather than choosing only one value.

The data structure should also support portability. Product records would be stored in structured formats such as JSON and CSV so that food suppliers, regulators, retailers, and researchers can transfer or reuse the information across systems. For example, a food recall record could include stable fields such as product_id, product_name, supplier, origin_location, processing_location, inspection_date, recall_status, allergen_warning, and source_url . This makes the information more reusable and interoperable instead of being locked inside one app. Similar to the example assignment, where users could download data in standardized formats and organizations could move information across systems, our project treats portability as part of availability rather than a separate technical feature.

Limitations

Our project has several limitations. First, the accuracy of the system depends on the quality and timeliness of data provided by suppliers, retailers, government recall databases, and inspection agencies. If a supplier delays updates, enters incomplete information, or uses inconsistent product names, the app may not immediately reflect the most accurate safety status. For example, can food products appear safe in the app even though a recall has recently been issued but not yet synchronized into the system.

Second, food supply chains are complex, and not every food item has the same level of traceability which means we can’t make sure all food products can be as detailed as we want. Packaged food may have clear batch numbers and manufacturer records, but fresh produce sold in bulk may have less detailed documentation. Small farms, local vendors, or imported products may also use different record formats, making integration harder. Even when the app uses structured JSON or CSV, inconsistent naming, missing fields, and different inspection standards can limit how complete the final information is.

Third, the app is not an official software published by the health department; the system cannot replace official regulatory judgment or professional food safety inspection which make it less reliable. It can help users identify risks, warnings, and available evidence, but it should not claim to guarantee that a food item is completely safe. Food safety can change during storage, transportation, or handling after the product leaves the original supplier. Therefore, the app should clearly state that its information is advisory and based on available records.

Ethics

Our project raises ethical questions because food safety information affects public health, consumer trust, business reputation, and regulatory accountability. From a virtue ethics perspective, the food inspection system should be designed around honesty, fairness, and public responsibility. We think the app we designed should not hide negative information to protect suppliers, nor should it exaggerate risk in a way that unfairly damages producers. A fair system should give users access to safety information while also giving suppliers a process to correct inaccurate records.

From a consequential ethics perspective, the project should maximize public benefit while minimizing possible harm. The benefit is that consumers can make safer food choices, regulators can communicate recalls more effectively, and retailers can respond faster to safety problems. However, potential harms include false alarms, misinterpretation of risk labels, or unequal impact on smaller suppliers who may lack resources to maintain high-quality digital records. Locke and Weber note that open data can create public benefit, but harm from disclosure, privacy risks, or unfair impacts can reduce or outweigh that benefit. Therefore, our system should include verification steps, clear source labels, update timestamps, and a correction request process.

From a deontological ethics perspective, the system must follow relevant food safety, privacy, labeling, and data protection rules. The app should clearly identify whether information comes from government recall databases, supplier reports, inspection records, or user-submitted reports. If the system collects user data, such as scan history, location, allergy preferences, or saved products, it should collect only what is necessary and explain how the data is used. Sensitive user information should not be sold or shared with advertisers. Locke and Weber explain that deontological ethics focuses on rules, laws, and institutional responsibilities, especially when data sharing involves privacy and public obligations.

The project should also address bias. If the app only includes large retailers or well-documented suppliers, it may make smaller vendors appear less trustworthy simply because they have less digital data. To reduce this bias, the system should separate “missing information” from “unsafe information.” A product with incomplete records should not automatically be labeled dangerous. Instead, the app should display a transparent status such as “limited information available” and explain what data is missing.

Societal Impact

The main positive societal impact of this project is that it can improve public access to food safety information. Many consumers do not know where their food comes from, whether it has been recalled, or whether it contains undeclared allergens. By connecting food items to portable digital records through QR codes, the system can make food safety information more visible and actionable. This can especially help people with allergies, families with children, older adults, and users who are more vulnerable to foodborne illness.

The project can also increase accountability across the food supply chain. Suppliers and retailers would have stronger incentives to maintain accurate records because consumers and regulators could more easily see product histories. Government agencies could also benefit from a standardized structure because recall warnings and inspection updates could be distributed faster. In this way, the system supports the broader open data value of using information to increase transparency, public trust, and community benefit.

The project may also create social risks. If the app is poorly designed, users may rely too heavily on a simple safety score without reading the details. This could create false confidence or unnecessary fear. There is also a risk that companies with more resources will appear more trustworthy because they can provide cleaner data, while smaller or local producers may appear less reliable because their records are less complete. To address this, the app should explain the meaning of each label, show data sources, and avoid ranking food products only by data completeness.

This project aims to make food safety information more portable, transparent, and useful. Its success depends not only on technical implementation, but also on responsible governance, clear communication, privacy protection, and fair treatment of all stakeholders. The app should be understood as a public-facing information bridge between consumers, suppliers, retailers, and regulators, not as a final authority that replaces official inspection or food safety law.
